Background

The quota system in Bangladesh was established to ensure representation and opportunities for underprivileged groups, including ethnic minorities, women, and economically disadvantaged individuals. Initially introduced to address disparities, it included various categories such as quotas for freedom fighters' descendants and indigenous peoples.

The Movement

The Quota Movement, which began in 2018, was driven by students from various universities who were dissatisfied with the existing quota system. They argued that the system was being misused and was not achieving its intended goals. The movement called for either the abolition or significant reform of the quota system, advocating for a shift towards a merit-based recruitment system.

Major Protests

In 2018, widespread demonstrations occurred, with students organizing rallies and sit-ins at universities, particularly Dhaka University. The government's initial resistance to the protests eventually led to concessions, resulting in a reduction of the quota from 56% to 10% for government jobs.

Outcome

The movement led to the government's decision to reform the quota system, significantly reducing the quota percentage and emphasizing merit-based criteria. Despite the changes, some activists continued to push for further reforms and greater transparency in the implementation of the new system.

Impact and Significance

The Quota Movement had a notable impact on the discourse surrounding social justice and employment in Bangladesh. It highlighted issues of fairness and the effectiveness of affirmative action policies, while also demonstrating the influence of student activism on national policy.

Challenges and Criticisms

Despite the reforms, there have been ongoing debates about the implementation and effectiveness of the new merit-based system. Additionally, the movement was intertwined with broader political dynamics, which sometimes colored perceptions of its goals and outcomes.
